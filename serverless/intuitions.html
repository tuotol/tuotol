The first time I deployed a serverless function, I felt a very delicious rush. My immediate reaction was "Wow, this is so easy!". I started web development around 2016 and right from the bat, I was overwhelmed by the sheer breath of tooling I had to familiarise myself before I could deploy anything non trivial. Serverless functions took a heavy weight off my shoulders. I have not voluntarily set up a web server since the first time I deployed a serverless function, except to learn FastifyJs and to sharpen my familiarity with MongoDb (I love document databases. What do I like about serverless functions? First, I like the clearer delineation of concerns, between the areas, forefront in my mind when I write any code(Business Logic and Site Reliability). My strategy is simple: each function encapsulates one unit of business logic. Put another way, I should be able to map each function directly to some value delivered directly to the user, where user here is a person or some intermediate or hidden software service.

Any sufficiently complex software system will need some notion of what is an acceptable level of availability. When I think of site reliability I think about availability and mean time to repair, fault tolerance(how do make sure components of the system fail without the whole thing crashing). I also think of three guiding principles: Eliminating toil, embracing risk and transparent service level objectives. The point of this word salad is that serverless functions make this significantly easier to reason about, plan for and execute. Whether it is GCP/Firebease, AWS or Azure, transparent SLAs from cloud service providers mean transparent SLAs for your system. Monitoring and observability work is stripped to actual monitoring and observability and much mundane setup and recursive maintenance work(everything you build to maintain must in itself be maintained). Product teams can c onsequently move a lot faster.

Later I will add more writing going into more details about my intuitions especially as update them with new experiences. My personal intuition is that serverless is inevitable for the industry as whole. Most importantly, I believe serverless significantly reduces the barrier to shipping quality, resiliant code and software. Developers can spend more time writing business logic and less time performing arcane swishes and swashes that only make sense to the Guild of Software developers.
